How it works — and why it's broken
The technology behind emotion recognition AI

EmoScan uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) trained on 1.2 million labeled facial images to classify expressions into 7 categories: happy, sad, angry, fearful, disgusted, surprised, and neutral.

The model was trained on predominantly white, young, neurotypical faces — meaning it performs significantly worse on everyone else.

Critically, it confuses expression with emotion. A focused student looks "angry." An autistic student looks "hostile." A shy student looks "disengaged." The system cannot know the difference.

Why does bias happen?

Training data bias: Most public emotion datasets (AffectNet, RAF-DB) are 60–70% light-skinned faces, predominantly from Western countries.

Cultural expression differences: Smiling norms, eye contact, and emotional display rules vary massively across cultures. AI trained on one culture fails on others.

Conflating expression with emotion: A focused person looks "angry." An introvert looks "disengaged." The model can only see muscle movement — not internal state.

No feedback loop: When the AI is wrong, there's no correction mechanism. Wrong decisions compound over time.

Real-World Impact

📌 Amazon Rekognition misidentified 28 US lawmakers as criminals, with higher error for people of color.

What is valid consent?

Informed: People must know exactly what is being scanned, why, and for how long data is kept.

Voluntary: "You must agree to be scanned to keep your job" is not voluntary consent.

Revocable: People must be able to withdraw consent at any time without penalty.

For minors: Children cannot legally consent. Parent/guardian consent is required — and even then, experts argue children should never be emotion-scanned in schools.
